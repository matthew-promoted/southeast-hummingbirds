## 1. Migration Timing by Region

### Overview
The **Ruby-throated Hummingbird** (*Archilochus colubris*) is the only hummingbird species that breeds in the eastern United States. Spring migration brings them north from Central America and Mexico; fall migration returns them south.

## 2. Species Identification Guide

### Primary Species: Ruby-throated Hummingbird
*Archilochus colubris*

The **only** breeding hummingbird in eastern North America. If you see a hummingbird in the Southeast from March–October, it is almost certainly a Ruby-throat.

#### Adult Male

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Throat (Gorget)** | Brilliant iridescent ruby-red; appears black in poor light, then "lights up" when sun hits at correct angle |
| **Crown** | Emerald green |
| **Back** | Metallic green |
| **Underparts** | Dusky gray-white on chest, grayish flanks |
| **Tail** | Forked, black |
| **Bill** | Black, straight, medium-length |
| **Size** | 2.8–3.5 inches (7–9 cm); Weight: 0.1–0.2 oz (2–6 g) |

**Image Source:**  
- Wikimedia Commons: [Male Ruby-Throated Hummingbird Hovering](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Male_Ruby-Throated_Hummingbird_Hovering.jpg) — CC BY-SA 4.0
- Macaulay Library / All About Birds: Adult male photos (credit Cornell Lab of Ornithology, fair use for educational purposes with attribution)

#### Adult Female

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Throat** | White or pale gray, sometimes with faint streaking; **NO red** |
| **Crown** | Green, duller than male |
| **Back** | Metallic golden-green |
| **Underparts** | White below with buffy/tan flanks |
| **Tail** | Rounded (not forked), black with white tips on outer feathers |
| **Bill** | Black, straight |

**Key Difference from Male:** No red throat, rounder tail with white tips, overall duller coloring.

**Image Source:**
- Wikimedia Commons: [Ruby-throated Hummingbird female](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Archilochus_colubris) — various CC licenses
- Unsplash: Free-to-use hummingbird photos (unsplash.com/s/photos/ruby-throated-hummingbird)

#### Juvenile

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **General Appearance** | Resembles adult female |
| **Throat** | May have faint dark streaking (green, black, or tan); juvenile males may show scattered red feathers appearing by late summer |
| **Tail** | Similar to female with white-tipped outer feathers |
| **Bill** | Slightly shorter than adults |

**How to Tell Juvenile Male from Female:**
- Juvenile males develop scattered red throat feathers by August–September
- Juvenile males have slightly more forked tails
- Females have more extensive gray coloring on cheeks

### Rare Winter Visitors

While Ruby-throated is the only breeding species, several western hummingbirds occasionally appear in the Southeast during fall and winter. **Keep feeders up through winter** to potentially spot these rarities!

#### Rufous Hummingbird
*Selasphorus rufus*

**Status:** Most common winter vagrant in Southeast (November–March)

| Feature | Male | Female/Immature |
|---------|------|-----------------|
| **Overall Color** | Bright orange-rufous | Green above, rufous flanks |
| **Throat** | Brilliant orange-red | Spotted, may have orange center |
| **Tail** | Rufous with dark tips | Rufous base, green/rufous mix |

**When to Expect:** October–March  
**Likelihood:** Uncommon but regular; most frequently reported winter hummingbird in GA, FL, SC, AL

**Image Source:**
- Wikimedia Commons: [Rufous Hummingbird](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Selasphorus_rufus) — CC licenses
- U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service: Public domain photos

#### Black-chinned Hummingbird
*Archilochus alexandri*

**Status:** Rare winter visitor

| Feature | Male | Female |
|---------|------|--------|
| **Throat** | Black with purple band at bottom | Plain whitish |
| **Overall** | Very similar to Ruby-throat | Nearly identical to female Ruby-throat |

**Key ID Point:** Males have black (not red) throat with purple base. Females nearly impossible to distinguish from Ruby-throat without careful examination.

**Image Source:**
- Wikimedia Commons: [Black-chinned Hummingbird](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Archilochus_alexandri)

#### Calliope Hummingbird
*Selasphorus calliope*

**Status:** Rare vagrant (1-2 reported per winter in Georgia)

| Feature | Male | Female |
|---------|------|--------|
| **Size** | Smallest bird in North America! | Same |
| **Throat** | Magenta streaks (not solid patch) | Faint streaking |
| **Tail** | Short, does not extend past wings when perched | Same |

**First recorded in Georgia:** Winter 1998–99

## 3. Feeder Recommendations

### Selection Criteria (Cleaning Focus)
The #1 factor in choosing a hummingbird feeder is **ease of cleaning**. Nectar ferments quickly in warm weather, promoting mold and bacteria that can harm hummingbirds. Choose feeders that:

1. **Disassemble completely** — No hard-to-reach crevices
2. **Have wide openings** — Easy to scrub inside
3. **Use simple designs** — Fewer parts = easier cleaning
4. **Include ant protection** — Built-in moat or compatible with add-on moats

### Cleaning Protocol (All Feeders)

| Frequency | Task | Method |
|-----------|------|--------|
| **Every 2-3 days** (summer) | Change nectar | Dump old, rinse, refill |
| **Every 5-7 days** (spring/fall) | Change nectar | Dump old, rinse, refill |
| **Weekly** | Deep clean | Hot water + bottle brush, no soap |
| **Monthly** | Sanitize | 1:10 bleach:water solution, rinse thoroughly |
| **As needed** | Mold removal | White vinegar soak 1-2 hours, scrub, rinse |

**Never use:**
- Dish soap (residue can harm birds)
- Scented cleaners
- Abrasive scrubbers on plastic (creates scratches where bacteria hide)

## 4. Homemade Nectar Guide

### The Only Recipe You Need

**Ratio: 4 parts water : 1 part sugar**

This matches the ~20% sugar concentration found in flowers naturally pollinated by hummingbirds.

| Batch Size | Water | White Sugar |
|------------|-------|-------------|
| Small | 1 cup | ¼ cup |
| Medium | 2 cups | ½ cup |
| Large | 4 cups | 1 cup |

### Instructions

1. **Measure water** — tap water is fine
2. **Add sugar** — plain white granulated only
3. **Stir until dissolved** — heating optional (see below)
4. **Cool completely** before filling feeder
5. **Store extra** in refrigerator up to 1-2 weeks

### Do I Need to Boil the Water?

**Short answer: No.**

The Smithsonian National Zoo's official guidance states: "The water for your nectar does not need to be boiled. Just be sure to stir or shake your mixture until the sugar is fully dissolved."

**Why some people boil:**
- Removes chlorine (minimal benefit)
- Helps sugar dissolve faster
- May slightly extend shelf life (debated)

**The science:** Any bacteria introduced during mixing will be overwhelmed by the high sugar concentration and rapid turnover (you're changing nectar every 2-3 days anyway).

### What NOT to Use

| Ingredient | Why It's Harmful |
|------------|------------------|
| **Red dye** | Unnecessary (feeder color attracts birds) and potentially harmful—no studies prove safety in hummingbirds |
| **Honey** | Promotes fatal fungal growth (Candida); ferments rapidly; can contain botulism spores |
| **Artificial sweeteners** | No nutritional value; birds may starve while feeling "full" |
| **Brown sugar / Raw sugar / Organic sugar** | Contains iron and other minerals that can be harmful to hummingbirds in high concentrations |
| **Powdered/confectioners' sugar** | Contains cornstarch and anti-caking agents |
| **Corn syrup** | Wrong sugar type; not what hummingbirds evolved to digest |
| **Fruit juice** | Ferments quickly; attracts insects; unnecessary |

### Why Plain White Sugar?

White granulated sugar is **pure sucrose**—the same sugar found in flower nectar. Hummingbirds have evolved specifically to digest sucrose efficiently.

"Raw" or "organic" sugars contain iron and molasses residues. While harmless to humans, hummingbirds cannot process excess iron, which can accumulate and cause organ damage over time.

### Nectar Freshness Chart

| Temperature | Change Nectar Every |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Below 70°F | 5-6 days |
| 70-80°F | 4-5 days |
| 80-90°F | 2-3 days |
| Above 90°F | 1-2 days |
| Cloudy/fermented | **Immediately** |

**Signs nectar has gone bad:**
- Cloudy appearance
- Floating particles
- Black mold spots
- Fermented/sour smell
- Stringy residue in feeder

## 5. Feeder Placement & Strategy

### Optimal Placement

| Factor | Recommendation | Why |
|--------|----------------|-----|
| **Sun exposure** | Partial shade preferred | Keeps nectar fresh longer; full sun = faster spoilage |
| **Visibility** | Visible from multiple angles | Birds need to find it; YOU want to watch it |
| **Height** | 4-6 feet off ground | Safe from ground predators; easy to maintain |
| **Distance from windows** | Either <3 feet OR >30 feet | Prevents fatal window strikes (see Section 8) |
| **Near perching spots** | Within 10-15 feet of trees/shrubs | Birds rest between feeding bouts |
| **Away from seed feeders** | 10+ feet apart | Reduces competition with larger birds |

### The Territorial Male Problem

Ruby-throated males are **extremely territorial**. A single dominant male will guard a feeder and chase away all others—females, juveniles, and rival males.

**Solutions:**

1. **Multiple feeders, spread apart**
   - Place feeders at least 15-20 feet apart
   - Better: out of sight of each other (around corners, opposite sides of house)
   - One bird can't guard what it can't see

2. **Cluster strategy**
   - Alternative: group 4-6 feeders tightly together
   - Too many ports for one bird to defend
   - Works well during peak migration when many birds pass through

3. **Mixed sun/shade locations**
   - Males prefer sunny, prominent positions
   - Place some feeders in partial shade for females and juveniles

### How Long Before Moving a Feeder?

**New setup:** Give a feeder at least 1-2 weeks in one location before moving. Hummingbirds need time to discover it and memorize its location.

**Established feeder:** Once birds are visiting, you can move it short distances (3-5 feet at a time) over several days. Birds will follow.

**Warning:** Moving a feeder to a completely new location may confuse returning birds. They have excellent spatial memory and will return to exact previous locations.

### Seasonal Placement Adjustments

| Season | Strategy |
|--------|----------|
| **Early Spring** (March) | Place in visible, sunny spot to attract scouts |
| **Peak Season** (May–August) | Add more feeders; spread out to reduce territorial fighting |
| **Fall Migration** (September–October) | Consolidate feeders; position near cover for tired migrants |
| **Winter** (if keeping feeders up) | Place in protected area; near house for frost protection |

## 6. Pest Control

### Ants

**The Problem:** Ants detect sugar from surprising distances. Once a scout finds your feeder, they'll form a trail and can quickly overwhelm it—drowning in nectar and contaminating it.

**Solutions (ranked by effectiveness):**

| Solution | How It Works | Pros | Cons |
|----------|--------------|------|------|
| **Ant Moat** | Water-filled cup above feeder; ants can't swim | Most effective; chemical-free | Must refill moat as water evaporates |
| **Built-in Moat Feeders** (HummZinger) | Same as above, integrated | Convenient; no extra purchase | Limited feeder options |
| **Permethrin Ant Guards** | Chemical deterrent on hanging mechanism | Long-lasting | Some prefer chemical-free |
| **Fishing Line** | Hang feeder from thin fishing line; ants can't grip | Free if you have line | Not as reliable |
| **Petroleum Jelly on Pole** | Sticky barrier | Cheap | Messy; can harm birds if they contact it; melts in heat |

**Important:** Never apply sticky substances, oils, or pesticides directly to the feeder itself.

**Recommended Product:** Aspects Nectar Guard Ant Moat (~$8) or feeders with built-in moats

### Bees, Wasps & Yellow Jackets

**The Problem:** Bees and wasps are attracted to the same sugar water that hummingbirds love. They can dominate a feeder and deter birds—or worse, sting them.

**Solutions:**

| Solution | How It Works |
|----------|--------------|
| **Choose saucer-style feeders** | Nectar level sits below ports; bee tongues can't reach it, hummingbird tongues can |
| **Use feeders with bee guards** | Mesh screens over ports block insects but allow bird bills |
| **Avoid yellow on feeders** | Bees associate yellow with flowers; choose all-red feeders |
| **Keep feeders clean** | Wipe drips immediately; sticky residue attracts insects |
| **Move feeder slightly** | Bees memorize exact locations; moving 3-4 feet can lose them while birds adjust quickly |
| **Use a 5:1 ratio temporarily** | More dilute nectar (5 water : 1 sugar) is less attractive to bees but still usable by hummingbirds |
| **Decoy feeder** | Place an extra-sweet feeder (3:1) away from bird feeders to lure insects elsewhere |

**Yellow Jacket Traps:** Consider placing commercial yellow jacket traps 20+ feet from feeders. Bait with meat or fish (not sugar) to avoid attracting bees.

**Sources:**
- Hummingbird Society: [How to Keep Bees and Ants Out of Feeders](https://www.hummingbirdsociety.org/post/how-to-keep-bees-wasps-and-ants-out-of-your-hummingbird-feeders)
- Birds and Blooms: [Keeping Bees Away from Feeders](https://www.birdsandblooms.com/birding/attracting-hummingbirds/keeping-bees-away-from-hummingbird-feeders/)

## 8. Window Strike Prevention

### The Problem

Up to 1 billion birds die annually from window collisions in the United States. Hummingbirds are particularly vulnerable because:
- They fly at high speeds (20-30 mph normal, faster when startled)
- They're drawn to feeders placed near windows
- Their small size makes even glancing strikes potentially fatal

### The Solution: The 3-Foot / 30-Foot Rule

**Place feeders either:**
- **Within 3 feet of windows** — Birds don't build up enough speed to injure themselves
- **More than 30 feet from windows** — Birds have time to recognize and avoid the glass

The **danger zone** is 4-29 feet from windows, where birds can reach full speed before impact.

### Making Windows Visible

If you have feeders in the danger zone or frequent window strikes:

| Solution | Effectiveness | Cost | Notes |
|----------|---------------|------|-------|
| **Window decals/stickers** | Good | $5-20 | Place 2-4 inches apart; birds see the pattern |
| **ABC BirdTape** | Excellent | $25-30 | American Bird Conservancy tested; apply strips 2-4" apart |
| **Acopian BirdSavers** | Excellent | $25-75 | Paracord hanging lines; very effective |
| **Tempera paint** | Good | $5-10 | Washable; create dots/patterns on outside of glass |
| **External screens** | Excellent | $50+ | Also reduces heat/glare |
| **UV-reflective glass** (Ornilux) | Excellent | $$$ | For new construction/replacement |
| **Netting** | Good | $20-50 | Install 2-3" from glass; birds bounce off safely |

**Key:** Patterns must be on the **outside** of the glass to be most effective. Interior curtains/blinds help but don't fully solve the problem.

### Window-Mount Feeders: A Double Solution

**Perky-Pet Window-Mount Hummingbird Feeder** (~$15)

These attach directly to the window via suction cups. Because birds are feeding AT the window, they:
1. Approach slowly and deliberately
2. Never build up collision speed
3. Give you incredible close-up views

**Recommended for:** Kitchen windows, home offices, anywhere you want to watch birds up close safely.

## 9. Seasonal Calendar

### Timing Guide for GA, FL, SC, AL

| Month | Activity | Tasks |
|-------|----------|-------|
| **January** | Off-season (or rare visitors) | Clean and store most feeders; keep 1-2 up for winter visitors |
| **February** | Early scouts arrive (South FL/GA) | Mid-month: Clean feeders, prepare nectar, put out 1-2 feeders |
| **March** | Migration begins | Week 1: Feeders up (Central FL, Coastal GA/SC); Week 2-3: Feeders up (North FL, Piedmont); Watch for first arrivals |
| **April** | Main arrival | Increase to 3-4 feeders; clean every 3-4 days; plant native flowers |
| **May** | Breeding season begins | Full complement of feeders; nesting behavior; increase cleaning frequency as temps rise |
| **June** | Peak breeding | Clean every 2-3 days; watch for fledglings; deadhead flowers |
| **July** | Peak activity | Highest feeder traffic; juveniles appear; clean every 2 days in heat |
| **August** | Early fall migration begins | Maintain high feeder count; migrating birds passing through |
| **September** | Peak fall migration | **Most important month** — may see 25-40 birds at once; don't reduce feeders |
| **October** | Late migration | Keep feeders up through month; stragglers still passing; rare western species may appear |
| **November** | Post-migration | Reduce to 1-2 feeders; watch for winter Rufous and other rarities |
| **December** | Winter | Maintain 1-2 feeders (especially coastal areas); rare visitors possible |

### Common Myth: "Taking Feeders Down Makes Hummingbirds Migrate"

**FALSE.** Hummingbirds migrate based on day length and internal biological clocks—not food availability. Leaving feeders up does NOT prevent migration and can actually help late-departing birds fuel up for their journey.

**Keep feeders up at least 2 weeks after you see your last hummingbird** to assist any stragglers.
